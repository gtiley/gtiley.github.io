Using Phylogenetics and Molecular Evolution to Understand 450 Million Years of Diversity

Estimating and Dating the Plant Tree of Life

I have often found phylogenetics for the sake of phylogenetics to be a somewhat boring venture. Molecular evolution studies that seek to test specific hypotheses are often more satisfying. However, there are a number of contentious relationships among major plant groups that require a deeper understanding into their contention to better investigate patterns of trait evolution, disparate diversification rates, and explaining why some plant groups how so many more species than others.

I have been happy to participate in large-scale phylogenomic efforts that not only seek to investigate phylogenetic relationships, but build the necessary resources for the scientific community to rigorously test phylogenetic hypotheses. These efforts are through 1KP 1 and GoFlag 2. These types of data are useful for a number of investigations into plant genome evolution 3

My research interests are currently focused on divergence time estimation 4. I am now using data generated from these large phylogenomic investigations to date the plant tree of life with multilocus nuclear datasets. Although there is a long legacy of divergence time studies in plants, there is a bit of a non-independence issue because nearly all investigations are based on the same chloroplast data mined from genbank. These data also provide an opportunity to demonstrate the effects of concatenation on divergence time estimation when incomplete lineage sorting is high, or when there are drastic shifts in life history traits or population size.

Although not my major focus, I am interested in the genome evolution of early-diverging plant groups and have contributed to moss genomics 5.
