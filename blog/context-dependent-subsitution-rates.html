Context-Dependent Substitution Rates

05 July 2020

Partitioning rates by substitution type

This was an area of research that I did not fully appreciate until our mouse lemur mutation rate project. A long legacy of mutation accumulation experiments and model organisms and more recent pedigree-based mutation rate studies have established that some types of mutations are more frequent than others.
For example, C->T transitions at CpG sites are largely due to deamination of methylated cytosines at CpG sites. If you could separate C->T transitions into those with a Guanine at their 5’ end versus those with anything except Guanine, you would probably find those at CpG sites outnumber all of the others. Thus, the “context” of the mutations is important and explains a lot of rate variation.
Molecular clock models are not only useful for estimating divergence times, but can be used to understand the evolution of the rate of evolution 1 among species. Standard phylogenetic clock models allow one to assign different sequences to difference partitions, which jointly estimates divergence times shared by all partitions as well as the evolutionary rate of each partition. Relaxed-clock models further allow a different rate for each branch in the species tree for each partition. This provides the necessary tools to lineage-specific variation in substitution rates across partitions.
Coincident with early genomes and when long, contiguous sequences from BAC chromosomes could be compared across several species, a number of studies proposed different solutions for estimating context dependent substitution rates on a phylogeny in the early 2000s. The ML density for a Markov model that treats n-tuples (such as dinucleotides or trinucleotides) as independent rate classes as well as the extensions to the pruning algorithm to estimate the ML was derived in 2004 2. As with many things in molecular evolution, Bayesian MCMC approaches can get around some of the computational difficulty and approximation needed for ML solutions. The foundation for the analyses done here are from Hwang and Green (2004) 3, where they estimated 96 different rate parameters assuming the complementary substituion types have the same rate (wxy -> wzy); the rate of x -> z depended on the flanking bases w and x. The authors were able to identify groups of parameters with similar rate estimates and importantly recognized that CpG -> TpG were more clock-like than any other substitution type, regardless of which base was on the 3’ side of the C. The clock like behavior of CpG -> TpG substitutions was tested in primates by Kim et al. (2006) 4 and later by Moorjani et al. (2016) 5 with additional implications for the importance of generation time for explaining relative substitution rates for other substitution types.
Important developments happend between 2004 and now. Specifically, there was the CAT model and Phylobayes 6, which uses mixtures of substitution models to account for among-site rate variation in a flexible way. An output of this Bayesian model is that one can generate a sample of stochastic character mappings for each site to estimate rates of evolution while considering uncertainty in the evolutionary history of each site (i.e. a CpG site was not always a CpG site after all). Lee et al. (2015) 7 used these stochastic character mappings to estimate the expected numbers of context-dependent substitutions and ultimately the substitution lengths. The substitution length estimates for each context-dependent substitution type can be used to estimate rates of evolution for each context-dependent substitution type under a relaxed clock with multidivtim 1.

Analyzing some primate data

Primates have been analyzed before, but because of the typical sampling biases pervasive in research, all strepsirrhines were excluded except as outgroups. Analyzing context-dependent substitution rates seemed like an interesting idea, because the mouse lemur mutational spectrum was … odd 8. Or rather unexpected compared to Anthropoidea. We actually found more C->T transitions not at CpG sites as opposed to CpG -> TpG transitions (Fig. 1A). The mouse lemur lineage-specific rates across substitution types do not find an elevated CpG -> TpG rate compared to C->T transitions at non-CpG sites (Fig. 1B).

Fig. 1 — Numbers of mutations and context dependent substitution rates for the gray mouse lemur. A) The number of mutations for each mutation type, including complements, based on a single pedigree of parents and their two offspring. B) Points are posterior means and error bars are credible intervals.

It gets more surprising! The CpG->TpG substitution rate for gray mouse lemur is actually quite low compared to other primates. Its CpG->TpG rate deviates from the expected clock-like behavior observed in great apes and the Old-World monkey Chlorocebus sabaeus. Although we also see a slightly elevated CpG-TpG rate in the New-World Monkey Callithrix jacchus(Fig. 2).

Fig. 2 — Context dependent substitution rates for the gray mouse lemur. Points are posterior means and error bars are 95% credible intervals.

I always try to break good results because I am pessimistic and assume interesting things I find can be explained by technical artifacts. We expect rates aside from CpG->TpG substitutions to be predicted largely by generation time. When we look at all of the other substitution types (Fig. 3), mouse lemur (~3.75 year generation time) has the second-highest rate after Callithrix jacchus (~2 year generation time). This leads me to believe the estimates are reliable and there is truly something interesting here. Resolving the genetic or other mechanisms that explain the difference is an issue beyond the scope of these methods.

Fig. 3 — Distributions of all nine context-dependent substitution types. Bar heights are posterior means and error bars are 95% credible intervals.

Tutorial

Required Software

  1. PhyloBayes MPI 9
  2. parsePhyloBayes.jar (https://github.com/HuiJieLee/ParsePhyloBayes)
  3. Multidivtime (https://brcwebportal.cos.ncsu.edu/thorne/multidivtime.html)

Input Data

  1. A phylogenetic tree
  2. Aligned sequences
  3. Example from mouse lemur 8 provided here

Getting usable input from Ensembl’s EPO whole-genome alignments

The Ensembl whole-genome alignments 10 are arguably better than something you could produce on your own. They are limiting in some sense that only well-annotated genomes hosted by Ensembl are available, but they have been used in previous studies of substitution rate variation 5 so we will use them again here. The alignments have rich synteny information in them, but that makes them very not usable for our purposes. Here is how I retrieved the alignments and reformatted them.
There might be slicker ways to retrieve data from Ensembl, but I prefer to simply go through the ftp site. Make sure you are downloading to an external drive or cluster because this will take a lot of space after decompressing.
On mac

cd 46_mammals.epo
curl -O ftp://ftp.ensembl.org/pub/release-100/emf/ensembl-compara/multiple_alignments/49_mammals.epo/*.epo.gz

Or Linux

cd 46_mammals.epo
wget ftp://ftp.ensembl.org/pub/release-100/emf/ensembl-compara/multiple_alignments/49_mammals.epo/*.epo.gz

Then decompress the epo files and extract with the ensembl’s emf2maf.pl script

gunzip *.gz
for i in *.emf
{
    perl emf2maf.pl $i
}

Go into the fasta directory, where we will get the maf formatted data (not sure what that is used for) into fasta format

cd ../fasta
perl formatFasta.pl

Then, we want to restrict our analyses to putatively neutral sites. I chose to go about this by masking all coding regions and cpg islands in the alignments based on annotations from the human genome. The distParts.pl script is simply a driver for maskFasta.subtype.pl. Normally, I would use this to distribute jobs on a cluster because it can take a little while. The output includes the directory noncoding where both coding regions and cpg islands are replaced with Ns. The directory noncpg is the same as noncoding, but cpg islands are removed rather than masked. That is because sequences in cpg islands goes to the cpg directory. I have not actually used the output from noncpg or cpg, but it seemed useful at the time if we want to explore the effects of hypermethylation of cpg islands on substitution rates.

cd ../neutralFasta
perl distParts.pl

Now, we just need to clean up a little after the masking and concatenate data for PhyloBayes. The masking generates a lot of columns that are only missing data, so I excise them here. The log and part file from concatenation give information about missing data and the partition boundaries for separate loci.

cd ../sitePatternFasta/noncoding
perl cleanAlignments.pl
perl concatenateFastaFiles.pl Mmur.noncoding.fasta Mmur.noncoding.log Mmur.noncoding.part

We are almost ready to generate some samples of stochastic character mappings! But first, the whole-genome alignment is huge and this will never run for this analysis. Maybe some of the steps will be useful for other divergence time investigations? So, I sampled ten 1Mb chunks from the concatenated alignment. Then, we run phyloBayes twice for each alignment, check convergence, and generated stochastic character mappings. Both chains should be run simultaneously on different nodes. The parsePhyloBayes.jar program (the jar file needs to be included in the same directory as the stochastic mappings) estimates the substitution lengths 7 and has a few arguments: 1000000 is the number of sites and 100 is the number of mappings. We use 100, which is low, because of the ludicrous amount of time needed for each step here. We were lucky to have 100 mappings after 2 weeks. That was after 2 months were dedicated towards the CAT-GTR runs. If I were to do this again, I would probably use shorter alignments or just treat each locus separately. Thus, keep in mind these analyses demand a lot of computation to get good posteriors. Otherwise, settle for smaller posteriors? You would get just as many mappings anyway, but actually observing convergence gives some confidence that there is information in the data.

cd ../siteRates
perl sampleNonCoding.pl
mpirun -np 16 pb_mpi -gtr -cat -dgam 4 -x 5 1200 -d mn.0.phy -T 7tax.tre pb01
mpirun -np 16 pb_mpi -gtr -cat -dgam 4 -x 5 1200 -d mn.0.phy -T 7tax.tre pb02
tracecomp -x 1000 pb01 pb02
mpirun -np 16 readpb_mpi -map -x 1000 pb01
java -jar parsePhyloBayes.jar 1000000 100 pb01 2 outgroup.txt
mv o.estb.* ../divtime/0.0
java -jar parsePhyloBayes.jar 1000000 100 pb02 2 outgroup.txt
mv o.estb.* ../divtime/0.1

The java step will have generated some input files for multidivtime - o.estb.group* files. We moved these into separate folders for multidivtime ananlyses. The fossil calibrations are justified in Campbell et al. (2020) 8 and the input trees required a slight modification. We can reformat output for Tracer, and generate some plots.

cd ../divtime/0.0
multidivtime multicntrl.dat > 0.0.stdout
cd ../divtime/0.1
multidivtime multicntrl.dat > 0.1.stdout
cd ../
perl mft.pl
R CMD BATCH PosteriorPlots.R

Of course any of your own analyses would require heavy editing to the plotting script and justifying options in the control file. This should give a rough guide to the steps for analyzing context-dependent substitution rates though.
